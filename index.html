Protocol as Terrain

A protocol is not inherently liberatory or oppressive. It is a terrain where power operates.

Consider three systems of distributed coordination:

THE HUMAN BODY runs on biochemical protocols. No CEO cell, yet highly organized. But this coordination makes you vulnerable: viruses exploit cellular handshakes, cancers weaponize growth signals. Standardization enables both function and attack.

THE INTERNET runs on technical protocols. no central authority, yet globally coordinated. But TCP/IP's openness enables mass surveillance, DNS can be captured by states. Decentralization doesn't mean democracy.

SOCIETIES run on social protocols—norms, rituals, unwritten rules. These enable coordination without constant policing, but they also enforce conformity and marginalize difference. Convention can be more controlling than law.

James C. Scott warned us about "seeing like a state", how legibility enables control. When systems become too readable, they become governable, but they also become brittle.

Galloway and Thacker showed us that power after decentralization operates through protocol, not despite it. Control doesn't require a center when the rules themselves do the work.

The question isn't whether protocols concentrate or distribute power... it's which protocols, serving whose interests, with what escape routes?

Observation 1: Legibility Should Be Consensual

You should control who can read you. Medical records legible to your doctor but not your employer. Financial transactions traceable for fraud prevention but not for behavior prediction.

Observation 2: Protocols Need Expiration Dates

Technical protocols ossify. Social protocols ossify. What starts as liberation becomes prison.

Watch: The Lifecycle of an Open Protocol

STAGE 1: Creation
Open standard created by researchers for academic exchange. Anyone can implement it. No gatekeepers.
STAGE 2: Adoption
Protocol becomes popular. Companies build on it. Network effects kick in. Users benefit from interoperability.
STAGE 3: Dominance
One or two implementations dominate. Switching costs increase. "Just use the standard" becomes "just use Google/Facebook/Amazon."
STAGE 4: Capture
Dominant players shape protocol evolution. Features added that benefit them. Alternative implementations starved of resources.
STAGE 5: Enclosure
What was open is now effectively closed. Users locked in. Innovation stifled. Exit becomes nearly impossible.

Examples in the wild:

• Email: Open protocol (SMTP) -> dominated by Gmail

• Web browsers: Open standards -> dominated by Chrome

• Social protocols: Open federation -> captured by platforms

THE ANT DEATH SPIRAL

Army ants follow pheromone trails.
Each ant reinforces the path for the next.

Sometimes they form a loop.
The trail gets stronger. More ants follow.
They walk in circles until they die.

The solution isn't to avoid protocols. It's to build them with designed impermanence; protocols that sunset, that require active renewal, that force renegotiation.

Three Observations for a Protocol-Saturated Future:

1. Monocultures are vulnerable by design

When one protocol dominates, a single exploit cascades everywhere. We need multiple, incompatible, overlapping protocols so that capture of one doesn't mean capture of all.

2. Legibility should be consensual and revocable

You should control who can read you. Selective opacity is not deception; it's boundary-making. The right to delete should be built into the protocol layer.

3. Protocols need expiration dates

Systems ossify. We need designed impermanence: protocols that sunset, that require active renewal, that force renegotiation of the terms.

The question is: Who gets to write the rules, and who gets written out by them?

Tools like ad blockers are just the beginning. They're tactical, short-term, important, but not enough.

The real work is learning to recognize systems that demand you fit a category, paths that look like freedom but lead in circles, optimization functions that don't include your survival.

Systems that leave room for what you actually are.
